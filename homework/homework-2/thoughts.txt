hw2 scratch notes

- the connections vs interactions distinction from hw1 keeps coming up. contrastive learning is explicitly about connections (data-level structure) - you're pulling matched pairs together without knowing anything about interactions yet. the fusion step is where interactions enter. albef's whole argument is just: don't skip to step 2.

- using waveform + mel-spectrogram as the two modalities was probably a mistake for contrastive learning specifically. they're not independent views, the mel IS derived from the waveform. contrastive learning works best when modalities carry partially non-overlapping signal (image + text, audio + video). here you're basically trying to align a signal with a transformation of itself, which means the model has no reason to learn anything interesting - the "cross-modal" structure is trivially there.

- the loss not decreasing (~3.1-3.4 throughout) is probably batch size as much as anything else. contrastive loss needs lots of negatives to work. with N=32 you only have 31 negatives per anchor. CLIP used 32k. that's not a hyperparameter tweak, that's a fundamental scaling issue.

- the cross-entropy framing is actually clarifying. N pairs, N×N similarity matrix, diagonal is correct matches - it really is just N-way classification. softmax makes scores comparative, which is what forces the geometry to organize. "why cross-entropy" is the wrong question, the better question is why the temperature parameter matters so much (controls how peaked the softmax is, too high = uniform distribution, too low = model overfits to hard negatives).

- platonic rep hypothesis: interesting but i'm skeptical it applies anywhere outside the "large scale diverse data" regime it was observed in. RAVDESS with 1440 clips is about as far from that as possible. the counter-arguments (modality-specific inductive biases, different architectures, task-specific shortcuts) feel more relevant at smaller scale.

- efficiency problem (P5) is the unsexy part but real - if multimodal fusion costs 3x the memory for 2% accuracy gain, that's a practical question not just a benchmarking exercise. the tradeoff depends entirely on deployment context.

- image >> audio on AV-MNIST (64.6% vs 41.4%) - audio CNN architecture isn't suited to spectrograms. spectrograms have time on one axis and frequency on the other, and those axes aren't interchangeable the way spatial axes are in image CNNs. a 1D conv over time or an architecture that respects the time-frequency structure would probably help more than any amount of dropout tuning.
